Backcombing, also known as teasing or ratting, is a technique that adds volume and height to the hair, creating that signature 80s look. So, if you're ready to rock that retro style, let's dive in!

The 80s era was known for its bold fashion choices, and hairstyles were no exception. Big hair was all the rage, and backcombing was the secret behind achieving that larger-than-life look. However, many people struggle with how to backcomb hair properly, resulting in tangled, messy hair instead of the desired volume. But fear not, because we're here to guide you through the process and help you master the art of backcombing!

So, how exactly do you backcomb hair 80s style? The first step is to section your hair and start with the bottom layers. Take a small section of hair and hold it straight up. Using a fine-tooth comb, comb the hair downwards towards the scalp in short, quick strokes. Repeat this process for each section, working your way up towards the crown of your head. Once you've backcombed all the sections, gently smooth the top layer of hair over the teased sections to create a polished look. And voila! You've successfully backcombed your hair 80s style!

In summary, to achieve the perfect 80s backcombed hairstyle, follow these steps: section your hair, backcomb each section from bottom to top, and smooth the top layer of hair over the teased sections for a polished finish. Practice makes perfect, so don't be discouraged if it takes a few tries to get the hang of it. With time and patience, you'll be able to rock that 80s look with confidence!

What is Backcombing and Why Was it Popular in the 80s?

Backcombing, also known as teasing or ratting, involves combing the hair towards the scalp to create volume and height. In the 80s, big hair was all the rage, and backcombing was the go-to technique for achieving that iconic look. The technique allowed individuals to create gravity-defying hairstyles that added drama and flair to their overall appearance.

The History and Myth Behind Backcombing

Backcombing has been around for centuries and has been used by various cultures throughout history. The technique was popularized in the 18th century during the Victorian era, where women would backcomb their hair to achieve elaborate updos. In the 80s, backcombing became a staple of the punk and new wave subcultures, with musicians and celebrities sporting voluminous hairstyles that defied gravity.

The Hidden Secrets of Backcombing

While backcombing may seem straightforward, there are a few hidden secrets that can make all the difference in achieving that perfect 80s look. One secret is to use a fine-tooth comb or a teasing brush specifically designed for backcombing. These tools help to create precise, controlled teasing without causing excessive damage to the hair.

My Recommendation for Backcombing Tools

Question and Answer:

Q: Can backcombing damage my hair?

A: Backcombing done correctly and with the right tools should not cause significant damage to your hair. However, excessive backcombing or using harsh tools can lead to breakage and damage over time. Be gentle when backcombing and use a fine-tooth comb or teasing brush to minimize damage.

Q: How long does backcombing last?

A: The longevity of backcombing depends on various factors, such as hair type, products used, and the technique used. Generally, backcombing can last for a day or two with the help of hairspray or other styling products. However, keep in mind that the teased hair may gradually lose volume and shape over time.

Q: Can I backcomb short hair?

A: While backcombing is typically associated with longer hair, it is possible to backcomb short hair as well. The key is to focus on smaller sections and be gentle to avoid tangling or damaging the hair. Experiment with different techniques and find what works best for your hair length and texture.

Q: How do I remove backcombing from my hair?

A: Removing backcombing from your hair is relatively simple. Gently comb through the teased sections using a wide-tooth comb or a brush. Start from the ends and work your way up to avoid causing any additional tangles or breakage. Once the hair is detangled, wash and condition your hair as usual to remove any remaining product.
